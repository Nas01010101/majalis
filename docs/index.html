The claim

A society steered by a model of itself, not by hand written rules

Agent societies today are steered by hand written heuristics. Majalis gives a society a learned model of its own shared cognition, and lets that model govern who acts, when they debate, and what gets committed.

Multi-agent debate on a single backbone mostly re-buys self-consistency at higher cost (arXiv:2502.08788, 2604.02460). The fashionable fix in the 2025-26 gating literature is a sparser rule: look at a fresh answer's confidence, decide fire or skip, move on. It works, and it also throws away the one thing a running society has that a single query does not: memory of its own state.

Majalis instead trains a world model of the society's shared belief board: it estimates which beliefs are wrong right now, forecasts which will be overturned next, predicts whether debating a given belief would actually help, and plans maintenance policies entirely inside itself before a token is spent live. That model, not a threshold someone guessed, decides whether a disagreement is worth its tokens, what to challenge, who argues, when to stop, and what gets written back.

It is the oldest open question in blackboard architectures (Hearsay-II, the 1970s: which knowledge source should fire next), answered with a learned model instead of a hand-written scheduler. The cost and accuracy numbers on this page are evidence for that claim, not the headline.

The society

Five roles, one writer, three backbones

A star topology: every role hands the orchestrator a typed artifact, never a free-form message to another agent, and the orchestrator is the only thing permitted to write to the board. That single design choice removes an entire failure class.

Orchestrator
deterministic Python

The only writer to the board. Assigns roles, routes the gate's fire-or-skip decision, dispatches and aggregates sub-checks, and commits every resolution. Star topology, zero free agent chat, dodges the inter-agent misalignment cluster in MAST's 2025 failure taxonomy (arXiv:2503.13657).

Extractor
qwen3.6-flash

Reads raw evidence and structures it onto the board as dated, sourced claims: who said what, when, and with what authority.

Proposer
qwen3.7-max

Answers questions from the board's current beliefs, instantly, never re-reading the raw evidence stream.

Skeptic
qwen3.7-plus

Attacks a doubted belief by decomposing the challenge into 2 to 4 binary sub-questions, ranked by expected information gain. Runs on a different backbone than the proposer and judge: author is not validator.

Judge
qwen3.7-max

Adjudicates from the key's provenance docket under an explicit source-authority policy, then rules and the fix is written back as a supersession, never a deletion.

Planner
qwen3.6-flash

Decomposes a composite committee resolution, a GO or NO-GO, into atomic sub-checks the orchestrator dispatches and aggregates.

The world model

Four organs, one model of a shared mind

"World model" is a claim we test, not a metaphor. Each organ ships as a small trained head, measured piece by piece, including where it fails.

01 · STATE ESTIMATION

What's wrong, right now

wrong_now: P(a belief on the board is incorrect). Trained on the society's own logged episodes, validated on boards it never saw.

0.937 AUROC on real LLM-built boards (0.999 in distribution). The hand-set gate it replaced scored 0.79.
02 · FORWARD DYNAMICS

What breaks next

superseded_next: a calibrated multi-horizon hazard curve, P(this belief is overturned within k evidence batches).

0.63 / 0.66 / 0.70 AUROC at k = 1, 2, 4. ECE under 0.01, zero monotonicity violations.
03 · ACTION-CONDITIONED OUTCOMES

Would debate actually help

P(correct | debate) trained on 592 real, mined (skip, debate) counterfactual pairs. Platt-calibrated, because the gate compares probability levels, not just ranks.

helps 4.6%, hurts 0/592 debate never once makes a shared board worse across 592 mined pairs
04 · PLANNING IN IMAGINATION

Auditioned before a token is spent

Under zero-latency serving, questions answered instantly from the board, repairs only between maintenance windows. Which repair policy earns the budget? The model auditions all of them inside itself, at $0.

92.2% → 99.5% learned-risk repair vs no maintenance, oracle reaches 99.9%; n = 1,600. The winning policy then ships live: 112/112 across 7 seeds, zero ask-time debates.

Two honest nulls, reported at full prominence

Null one. A "planned" gate that looks two branches ahead, weighting the skip-versus-debate decision by the hazard forecast, never beats the plain reactive threshold gate. It matches it, 320/320 correct, at nearly twice the spend ($0.0059/q against the reactive gate's flat $0.0049 to $0.0054/q). The extra lookahead bought nothing.

Null two. Discounting maintenance-window repair priority by the hazard curve, instead of the raw wrongness estimate, never beats simple myopic risk repair. Both land at 99.5% on the same 1,600 held-out questions. At the horizons this society operates over, the model's decision value concentrates in calibrated state estimation, not long-horizon planning. We report that because it is true.

GSM8K: a recognized external benchmark

GSM8K has no belief board and no supersession, so the gate was honestly adapted rather than reused as-is: majalis-gated reads verbalized confidence off the same call that produces the answer, and only samples k=2 self-consistency votes as a tiebreaker below 0.85 confidence. No learned world model, no conformal calibration; GSM8K has no held-out calibration split, so this gate is an explicit, uncalibrated heuristic.

MMLU: is a full debate ever worth its cost?

GSM8K asks whether the gate keeps up with a single agent. MMLU asks the question a judge actually cares about: would you have been better off just running a normal multi-agent debate? Field notes

Building a world model for a council of agents

By Anas Elghoudane Qwen Cloud Hackathon 2026 ~9 min read

Why debate needed a governor

We started, like most people wrestling with agent societies in 2025 and 2026, staring at a paper that stung: on a single model backbone, multi-agent debate mostly re-buys self-consistency at ten times the cost. Sample the same model a few times, take a vote, and you get most of what a full debate gets you for a fraction of the price. That finding kills a certain kind of demo, three agents arguing dramatically on stage, and it should. But it also asks a sharper question that the "gate per query" literature of the last year mostly ducks: if debate is rarely worth it, when exactly is it worth it, and who decides?

The 2025-26 answer, from DOWN to iMAD to SELENE to ARMOR-MAD, is a stateless classifier: look at a fresh answer's confidence, decide fire or skip, move to the next question. It works, and it also throws away the one thing a running agent society actually has that a single query does not: memory. A society answering the fortieth question of a session already knows things it didn't know at question one. A per-query gate can't use that. We wanted a gate that could.

From heuristic to model

The earliest version of Majalis was, honestly, a heuristic: a hand-tuned blend of source disagreement, source trust, and staleness, thresholded to decide fire or skip. It worked well enough to survive as an ablation you can still run today (MAJALIS_WM=heuristic). But a heuristic is a set of weights someone guessed once and rarely revisits. So we logged the society's own episodes, every belief it ever asserted, every debate it ever ran, every outcome, and trained on them instead.

That pivot is what this page is built around. The gate isn't a rule anymore, it's a model. wrong_now is a small trained head that estimates P(this belief on the board is wrong right now), and we can tell you exactly how good it is: 0.999 AUROC on data it trained on, and more importantly, 0.937 on real LLM-built boards it had never seen during training. The heuristic it replaced scored 0.79 on the same held-out test.

A model of the whole council, not just the doubt

Once you have a trained estimate of "is this wrong right now," the natural next question is "what happens next." We built a second head, superseded_next, that forecasts a calibrated hazard curve: the probability a belief gets overturned within the next 1, 2, or 4 evidence batches. It isn't spectacular, AUROC climbs from 0.63 at k=1 to 0.70 at k=4, but it's honest and it's calibrated (expected calibration error under 0.01), and its risk estimate never inverts across every horizon we tested.

Then we asked a harder question still: does debating actually help, on this specific belief, right now? We mined 592 real counterfactual pairs from the society's own transcripts, cases where we know what happens if you skip and what happens if you debate the same belief, and trained an action-conditioned outcome head on them. The honest number: debate helps on 4.6% of questions and never once hurts, 0 out of 592. Most of the time skipping was already correct. The model earns its keep on the sliver where it isn't.

The last organ is the one we're proudest of: planning in imagination. Once you can estimate state, forecast risk, and predict the outcome of an action, you can audition policies without spending a token. Under zero-latency serving, questions answered instantly from the board, repairs saved for maintenance windows, which policy should get a fixed repair budget? We ran the question 1,600 times inside the model alone, at $0: doing nothing holds 92.2% accuracy, ranking repairs by learned risk lifts that to 99.5%, and an oracle that can see the future tops out at 99.9%. Ninety-five percent of the reachable gap, closed before a single live call. Then we shipped the winning policy for real: majalis-maintain runs it live and holds 112 out of 112 across seven seeds with zero debates at ask time.

The two nulls we kept

A world model you only report when it flatters you isn't a model you can trust.

Two results from this project didn't work, and they're on this page at the same size as the wins.

First: we built a "planned" gate that looks two branches ahead, weighting the skip-versus-debate decision by the hazard forecast instead of just the current-wrongness estimate. It never beats the plain reactive gate. It matches it, 320 out of 320 correct, at nearly twice the cost ($0.0059 per question against the reactive gate's flat $0.0049 to $0.0054). The extra lookahead bought nothing.

Second: we tried discounting maintenance-window repair priority by the hazard curve instead of the raw wrongness estimate. It never beats simple myopic repair, ranking by "how wrong is this right now" and fixing the worst offenders first. Both land at 99.5% on the same 1,600 held-out questions.

Read together, the two nulls say something specific about this environment: at the horizons this society actually operates over, the decision value is concentrated in knowing what's wrong right now, calibrated state estimation, not in forecasting further ahead. That's a real finding, and it tells us where to spend the next round of modeling effort, better state estimation rather than deeper lookahead, which is exactly what a world model is supposed to be for.

What ships, what's next

The productized core of all this is boring in the best way: belief-base hygiene for any evolving corpus. A knowledge base, a CRM, a threat-intel feed, anything that accumulates conflicting claims over time. majalis-maintain repairs it in windows at 112 out of 112 with zero ask-time debates. The conformal accept bound, E[error given committed] at most alpha, checked empirically at 2.1% against a 5% target on 1,600 held-out questions, is the kind of guarantee a platform team can actually put in an SLA. And the live /ingest and /ask API on the deployed box will take any evidence stream you feed it, right now, no code required.

Limits

What we don't claim

1

The conformal guarantee is marginal over exchangeable tasks and applies to the ACCEPT decision only: it bounds error on beliefs committed without a debate, not on every answer the society ever gives.

2

Our benchmark families are synthetic but property-tested: gold answers are provably consistent with the latest authoritative evidence. GSM8K exists on this page precisely to check the story holds on a widely-cited external benchmark, but its gate is an explicit, uncalibrated heuristic with no held-out calibration split, not the full conformal machinery.

3

One residual, non-weak error class, roughly 2.5% of questions, sits inside the calibrated band and is not eliminated by gating alone.

4

The planned gate never beats the reactive gate, and hazard-discounted maintenance never beats myopic risk repair. Both nulls are reported at full prominence above, not buried.
